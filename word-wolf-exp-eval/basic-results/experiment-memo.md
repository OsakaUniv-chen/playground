Targeting analysis — final results (all 50 bags, 40,164 ticks)
A. Four-label ratio (ground-truth acoustic map)
Overall: Left 25.6% / Right 23.5% / Teleoperator 31.3% / Others 19.6%. Distributions are similar across all four modes (the acoustic scene is the same; only the gaze policy differs).

B/C. PSSP +1 s prediction vs persistence baseline
PSSP	Persistence	Δ
4-label	0.464	0.477	−0.012
L/R only	0.461	0.449	+0.012
The pattern is consistent across every mode. The SimVP +1 s prediction is at parity with — marginally below — the trivial "keep the last frame" baseline. It does slightly better only on the actionable Left/Right subset. Confusion-wise, Teleoperator is predicted best (recall 0.59), Others worst (0.27). Takeaway: predicting the sound map +1 s ahead adds essentially no value over just looking where the sound is now.

D1. Gaze tracking — does the robot look at the acoustic speaker?
mode	gaze-on-speaker	κ	gaze P(L)
PSSP	0.671	0.342	0.472
DoA	0.656	0.309	0.449
Tele	0.577	0.160	0.352

This is the clean, headline result. PSSP and DoA gaze track the acoustic speaker well above chance (κ ≈ 0.31–0.34); Random sits at chance (κ ≈ 0.04); Tele is intermediate (κ ≈ 0.16). Random ≈ 0 validates the metric (random gaze shouldn't track the speaker). Honest caveat: DoA/PSSP gaze is acoustically derived, so high alignment is partly mechanical — but that's exactly the point: they target the speaker, Random does not, even though both have near-identical switch dynamics (per your earlier behavior-similarity analysis).

What this means for the report (§3.2-1 → §3.5)
This resolves the open question. PSSP selects the correct (acoustic) target far more than Random (κ 0.342 vs 0.041) — they differ sharply in target selection despite near-identical switch dynamics. Combined with the subjective null (PSSP ≈ Random in perception), this is Branch A: semantically correct social gaze is not perceived — evaluation is dominated by motion dynamics, not who the robot looks at. That's the strong "perception mechanism" paper outcome.

κ (Cohen's kappa)
It measures how well the robot's gaze side (Left/Right) matches the acoustic speaker side (Left/Right), corrected for the agreement you'd expect by pure chance given how often each side occurs.

$$\kappa = \frac{p_o - p_e}{1 - p_e}$$

p₀ = observed agreement = fraction of L/R ticks where gaze side == speaker side. This is exactly the gaze-on-speaker number in D1.
pₑ = chance agreement = P(gaze=L)·P(speaker=L) + P(gaze=R)·P(speaker=R), from the marginal frequencies.
Reading: κ = 0 → agreement is no better than chance; κ = 1 → perfect; κ > 0 → genuinely tracking.

Why it matters here: a raw rate can be inflated by base rates. Random has gaze-on-speaker 0.514 but κ = 0.041 ≈ 0 — it "agrees" half the time only because there are only two sides, not because it's tracking anyone. PSSP has 0.671 → κ = 0.342, i.e. real tracking well above chance. κ is what lets you compare modes fairly.

gaze P(L)
The robot's overall left/right gaze balance, from /boxie/boxie_motors yaw sign:

$$\text{gaze P(L)} = \frac{#(\text{yaw} > 0)}{#(\text{yaw}>0) + #(\text{yaw}<0)}$$

yaw > 0 = turned to the left participant, yaw < 0 = right, yaw = 0 excluded. 0.5 = perfectly balanced; higher = more left-biased. It's purely where the head pointed, ignoring who was speaking (same quantity as P(L) in your report's appendix C-2).

D2 — "following a speaker switch"
D1 answers a static question ("on average, is the robot aimed at the speaker?"). D2 answers a dynamic one: when the speaker changes person, does the robot turn to follow, and how fast?

How it's built:

A switch = the ground-truth acoustic speaker flips sides (Left→Right or Right→Left). #switch = how many flips occurred in that mode's bags.
For each switch, watch the robot gaze for the next 3 s. If the gaze reaches the new speaker's side within 3 s, that switch counts as followed.
The columns:

#switch — number of speaker changes observed.
follow<3s — fraction of switches the robot caught up to within 3 s. Higher = more responsive.
already0 — of the followed switches, the fraction where the gaze was already on the new side at the moment of the switch (latency = 0). High = the robot was often already there (anticipation, or coincidence).
med_lat>0 / mean_lat>0 — the median/mean catch-up time in seconds, computed only over switches that actually required a move (latency > 0). I exclude the already-aligned ones so the 0's don't drag the latency artificially to zero (that's why the earlier "median 0.00" was uninformative).
